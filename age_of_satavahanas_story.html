Kingship

The Satavahana polity followed the rule specified in the Sastras, and it adhered to the sovereign. Monarchy in the state was hereditary, and even though the Satavahanas did not mention their fathers in their inscriptions that primarily bore matronymics, succession was always reckoned on patriarchal lineage. The King was the commander in war and led his armies personally to the battlefield. The detailed instructions issued to Amacas (Amatyas, Governors of Provinces) bear testimony to the effective control that the King had over his officers in every region of his empire; and the actions of the kings were not limited to issuing orders but made sure to take the necessary steps for realising their intentions. It implied that the King was extremely powerful in simple administrative machinery such as the Satavahana one, both in theory and practice. As a guardian of the religious and social order, his ‘fearless hand’ was to be ‘wet by the water poured out to impart fearlessness.’ The King was known to ‘sympathise with the problems of his citizens’ and ‘never employ taxes except as a practice of justice.’ He should properly utilize time and the space for the betterment of all his subjects. A king who was well aware of the perception of his subjects would be more than human if he escaped the obsession of his position as a king and ruler. Moreover, he was always aided and advised by his ministers, some of whom were confidential (Visvasya Amatya).

Provincial and Local Administration

Maharathis were hereditary governors of provinces, and they had the rank and power that was far superior to those of ordinary governors or Amatyas who were in charge of districts. Whilst Amatyas were involved in grants of lands and villages to religious bodies under specific commands of the sovereign, the Maharathi, like a feudal vassal, grants villages with the fiscal immunities attached to them in his name. Mahasenapatis was another dignitary in the empire, next above the rank of the Senapati. The Mahasenapatis have non-military duties, but this confusion of functions though it may seem curious to modern, was a common feature in Indian polity. The Samdhivigrahika is sometimes used in connection with Mahadandanayaka, which is associated with the great feudatory titles of Mahasenapati, Maharaja, Mahapratihara, and Mahasamanta.

Except for a few districts where feudatories held their sway, the empire was divided into Aharas and each Ahara. The next division below that of Ahara is Gama (Grama). From the Saptasatakam of Hala, we understand that the officer in charge of the village was called Gamika (Gramika). Other functionaries known to us are the Mahatarakas, Maha-aryakas, Bhandagarikas, Heranikas, Mahamatas in charge of Buddhist monks, the Nibamdhakaras or officers in charge of the registration of documents (the Aksapa-talikas of later times), the Pratiharas, the Dutakas who carried royal orders, and the Amacas in charge of drafting royal orders.
