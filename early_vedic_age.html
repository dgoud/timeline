1500- 1000 B.C.

Early Vedic Age

The earliest literature in India, the Rigveda, provides meagre yet significant information about the polity of the Early Vedic.The administration during Rig Vedic period was at three levels; The Graha (house) headed by the Grhapati or Jaspati, Vis headed by the Vispati and Rastra (area) headed by the Rajan respectively. Interestingly, there are two kinds of States discussed in Rigveda: (i) State with a tribal character (ii) an evolved State with a territorial kingdom, known as the term Rastra. The Vaksukta1 refers to the presence of Rastra. However, the term Rastra in this period was significantly different from that of the Later Vedic period.

During this early period, people were organised into tribes, not kingdoms, and the chief of a tribe was called Rajan. , whose primary duty was protecting the tribe. The term Rajan of the Rigvedic period was not a monarch of the kind we find in later Vedic period with the definite territory, powerful army, etc. He also did not use lofty titles and was called Gopati and Vispati. The rajan had limited power as he had to consult tribal councils called Sabha and Samiti. These two bodies were in part responsible for the governance of the tribe. The Rajan could not accede to the throne without their approval.

Sabha was a council of elders and advised the king on administration. It also functioned as a court of law and tried criminal cases. The head of the sabha was known as ‘Sabhapati’. Samiti was a general assembly of people, and its head was known as ‘pati’. The Samiti mainly dealt with the political business of the state. Another such assembly was Vidhatha, meant for economic, social and military purposes.
