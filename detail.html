BACK TO HARAPPAN CIVILISATION

What do the Excavated Materials Indicate?

The salient features of the Harappan civilization include very well-planned cities, residential and public architecture, a water management system, seals, unique script, uniform weights and measures, black on red pottery, ornaments, faience, bronze artifacts, sculptures, figurines, etc. It is also known for its prosperity, uniform culture, production of crafts, and internal and maritime trade. All these are indicative of a functional state. The procurement of raw materials from far-flung places, specialization in craft production, and the distribution of goods and materials over an extensive area also point toward a well-established trade which can only be possible in an efficient administrative setup. Some of the unearthed structures in Harappa, Dholavira, and Mohenjodaro seem to qualify as administrative buildings. SOURCE

Seals as Administrative Instrument

The standardization of weights and inscribed devices indicate that Harappans had intricate administrative machinery. Rajesh P. N. Rao opines that a probable function of Harappan seals and tablets was to record rations allocated to porters. Instead of signifying ownership of property, he argues that these seals might have been used for economic transactions. Jane Mcintosh points out that the availability of vast natural resources must have been very crucial for promoting peace within the Indus civilization, which had no pressure on resources and no incentive to struggle. A related detail is found in the arguments of Asko Parpola and Akinori Uesugi. They said that along with the proliferation of Kot Diji culture, we observe a growing use of a new type of stamp seal. The parallel between the use of this seal and the emergence of the mature Harappan period appears quite interesting if we accept this seal as an instrument of administration.

From the seals, we also get to know that the artisans and craftsmen were provided opportunities which shows there was a close integration between economic and social processes. This is the mark of a successful administrative machinery. SOURCE

Centralisation and decentralization

Earlier archaeologists and historians envisaged an empire with high centralization and robust administrative machinery. What is definite is that a state with some centralization did exist in the Harappan civilization considering the uniform weight and measures, the size of bricks, and the street layouts. There was a well planned layout and the cities were governed efficiently. There is no evidence of a key committee looking after each city. Also the committees that existed, consisted of only rich merchants and priests. Moreover, the script of the Harappan culture remained unchanged throughout their history, and new houses were built almost exactly at the same site as the old with only minor variations in the planning of the houses, and the street plans remained unchanged; it appears that there was a continuity in governance throughout the civilization.
